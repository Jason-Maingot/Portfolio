Stress Generator

A VR Rope Bridge Simulation to Measure Biological Stress Responses

A view of the project in the Unity editor

Overview

 Stress Generator is a virtual reality simulation developed in collaboration with the University of Miami’s Virtual Experience and Simulation Lab (VESL) and Dr. Firdaus Dhabhar, a tenured professor in the Department of Psychiatry and Director of the Laboratory of Stress and Resilience in Health and Healing.

 The goal of this project is to simulate a precarious rope bridge crossing, allowing researchers to trigger and study participant stress responses using the HP Omnicept headset. The system outputs biometric data and researcher annotations for later analysis.

My Role: Project Lead & Lead Developer

 I joined the project as the Project Lead and primary developer, taking over from a previous group of student developers. My responsibilities included:

  • Planning and managing development sprints
  • Organizing regular check-ins with the PI
  • Refactoring and upgrading core systems
  • Implementing new features and improving existing functionality
  • Ensuring the simulation was polished and stable for researcher use

Revamping the Environment and Researcher UI

A comparison of the old environment (left), and the new environment (right).

The first phase of my work focused on two major areas:

1. Participant Environment:
 The original bridge environment was too short, visually unconvincing, and lacked depth perception. Based on feedback from the PI, I:

  • Redesigned the canyon to be wider and deeper, improving its psychological impact
  • Introduced a more natural, less arid environment with a river, boulders, and foliage to enhance immersion
  • Added spatial audio for realism and depth
  • Balanced visual perspective to amplify height perception

2. Researcher Interface:
 A Figma-based design was provided to modernize the researcher UI. I:

  • Recreated the UI in Unity with full interactivity
  • Integrated biometric data display and note-taking functionality
  • Ensured researcher annotations are saved alongside the simulation data for analysis

A picture of the full design for the UI on Figam

Advanced Environmental Controls

To allow researchers greater control over participant experience, I implemented a suite of dynamic environmental tools, including:

  • Time of day: Toggle between day and night
  • Weather effects: Switch between clear and rainy conditions
  • Fog and wind levels: Adjustable in real-time
  • Dynamic canyon depth: Alter perceived height mid-simulation
  • Bridge Instability: Trigger falling rocks and breaking planks for heightened tension

 I also iterated on a feature where researchers could break bridge planks mid-crossing, introducing surprise elements to amplify stress. One major technical challenge involved ensuring Unity’s XR Toolkit properly synced physics between the participant and moving bridge elements, especially under strong wind forces.

A view of the simulation in progress, with fog and rain settings on, and planks broken in front of participant

Enhancing Participant Mobility

 Originally, movement across the bridge was joystick-based. Based on a new requirement from the PI, I added support for real-world physical locomotion using the HP Omnicept controllers as trackers.

This involved:

  • Researching motion tracking options compatible with the Omnicept
  • Reworking the movement system to accommodate step-based locomotion
  • Allowing researchers to select between joystick and physical movement modes
